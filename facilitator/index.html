Facilitator Notes

The teaching guide for Module 1: Claude for Beginners. Section-by-section moves, likely questions, and what NOT to teach.

Before the session

  • Open the module HTML on your laptop and test the access code (default: BLOOM2026)
  • Test each section unlock code (listed below) to confirm they work
  • Send attendees the link + access code only — not the section codes
  • Confirm everyone has a free Claude.ai account created in advance
  • Have your own Claude open in a separate tab to demo from
  • Start your audio recording before you say hello
  • Optional: have one of your real builds ready to show
Quick admin

To change the access code: open the HTML in any text editor, find const ACCESS_CODE = 'BLOOM2026'; near the bottom of the script, change the value, save.

To change section unlock codes: find each data-code="..." attribute on the <section> tags. Change as you wish. Keep them short and easy to spell.

Section unlock codes

Words you announce verbally during the session. Attendees type them into the lock card on each section. Until you give the code, the section stays locked — no clicking ahead.

Recording the session

For Zoom sessions

  • Use Zoom's built-in cloud or local recording. Both audio and screen are captured.
  • For transcription, Otter.ai integrates directly with Zoom and gives you a searchable transcript automatically.

For in-person sessions

  • iPhone Voice Memos is fine for audio-only. Place phone in the middle of the table.
  • Otter.ai mobile app gives you live transcription as the session happens.
  • For better fidelity: Rev.com or Descript record + transcribe with speaker labels.

Section-by-section teaching moves

01 Welcome
5 minno code
  • Open with their why — these are women who saw your apps and wanted in. Honor that.
  • Walk them through the setup checklist on screen. Don't move on until everyone has all four boxes checked.
  • For first-timers: "Some of you have never opened Claude before. You are exactly where you should be."
  • For returners: "Some of you have already played around. Stay anyway — there's a section on where your work actually lives that almost nobody gets right."
  • Land the closing line: "You are learning a working relationship with a very capable collaborator."
  • Then announce: "The code for the next section is SAGE."
02 How Claude Works
5 minSAGE
  • Keep this short. The mental model matters; the technical details do not.
  • Read the four bullets aloud. Expand on "it can be wrong" with one quick example.
  • Don't get pulled into AI ethics, training data, or "is it conscious." Note the question, defer.
  • Then: "The code for the cheat sheet is MOSS."
03 Mac & PC Cheat Sheet
3 minMOSS
  • Don't read every row. Land the framing — "the magic key on PC is Ctrl, on Mac is Cmd, and most shortcuts only swap that one key."
  • Point out that the system auto-detects which computer they're on and highlights their column.
  • Spend 30 seconds on screenshots — call it their "secret weapon" with Claude.
  • If you have switchers in the room: "this is the part I personally got lost on the first time."
  • Then: "The code for the home screen is WILLOW."
04 Home Screen Tour
10 minWILLOW
  • Share your screen. Walk through every part of the sidebar in order, then the in-chat elements.
  • Call out Projects especially — this is the feature that turns Claude from a chatbot into a real workspace.
  • Give them two minutes of silence to click around their own Claude. Set a timer. Don't talk during it.
  • Reconvene with: "What did you find that you didn't know was there?"
  • Then: "The code for the next section is BIRCH."
05 Coding Capabilities
10 minBIRCH
  • The point: take the mystery out of "what can I do with Claude." Lower the stakes.
  • On Claude Code: mention briefly, then "You don't need this yet." Repeat: not yet.
  • On modes: "different ways to use Claude." Most will only ever use the regular chat plus occasional Research.
  • Then: "The code for the hosting section is HARBOR."
06 Where Builds Live
10 minHARBOR
  • Most conceptually slippery section. Slow down.
  • Use the analogies in the module — sketch / public address / professional address.
  • If someone asks about domains and DNS — note it exists, defer the details.
  • If someone has heard of Vercel or Netlify — affirm them as similar. Don't list more options.
  • Then: "The code for our practice section is BLOOM."
07 Hands-On Practice
15 minBLOOM
  • This is where they actually do it. Stop talking. Walk around (or watch in Zoom).
  • Common stuck point: they over-think the opening prompt. "Just press enter. You can change it."
  • Second stuck point: they forget how to paste between tabs. Refer them to Section 03.
  • About 10 minutes in, prompt them to try a refinement. Don't let anyone get stuck on version one.
  • At the 14-minute mark: "Wrap up where you are. Last code coming."
  • Then: "The code for the closing section is GROVE."
08 Beyond Basics
5 minGROVE
  • Keep it light. Plant the seed; don't teach Supabase.
  • The most useful single line: "You learn Supabase through Claude, not before Claude."
  • End on the closing wisdom — read it aloud. It lands.
  • Final move: point them to the session log card. "If you can spare thirty seconds, please tap that button and paste the result into a reply to my email. It helps me make the next module better."

Likely questions + suggested answers

Is the free version of Claude enough?

For everything in this module, yes. If you want longer chats, more uses per day, and access to the most capable model (Opus), upgrade to Pro. Don't need to upgrade today.

Can Claude get me in legal trouble for using its work?

Anthropic gives you ownership of your outputs. That said, if you ask it to copy someone else's website, that's still copying. Common sense applies.

What about ChatGPT? My [husband / colleague] uses that.

Both are fine tools. Claude tends to be stronger at sustained coding work and is what we're learning today. You can use both.

Can it build me a real app like Erika's?

Yes — and I did, with no prior coding experience. It takes more than one hour. This is the start.

How much does it cost to put my page on GitHub or Cloudflare?

Both have generous free tiers. You can build and host real things without spending anything until you're ready to add a custom domain (~$10–15/year).

What if Claude makes a mistake in my code?

You ask it to fix it. "This isn't working — the button doesn't do anything when I click it." Describe what's broken in plain English. Iteration is the work.

Will AI replace [my work / my industry]?

Honest answer, kept short: it changes a lot. The people who learn to direct it well do better than the people who don't. That's why you're here.

Why can't I see all the sections at once?

Because we're walking through this together. I'll share each code as we get there. (Then move on. Don't apologize for the structure.)

What NOT to teach in this module

Save these for follow-up sessions:

  • API keys, pricing tiers in detail
  • Claude Code installation walkthrough
  • Git, branching, version control
  • Custom domains and DNS
  • Supabase actually being set up (mention only)
  • Stripe integration
  • Anything about prompts as a "system" or prompt engineering frameworks
The whole point

The goal is a felt sense of "I can do this," not a complete map.

The session log feature

At the end of the module, after Section 08 (Beyond Basics) is unlocked, a "Share Your Session With Erika" card appears for each attendee. It contains:

  • Their name and date
  • Time they entered, time of last activity, total session time
  • Every section they unlocked, in order, with timestamps
  • Failed unlock attempts (where they tried to click ahead)
  • Prompts they copied (engagement signal)
  • Time spent on each section
  • Setup checklist items they checked

They click Copy log to clipboard and paste it into a reply email to you. To collect logs, point everyone to the card at session end and say: "If you can spare 30 seconds, copy this and paste it into a reply to my email. It helps me make the next module better."

After the session

  • Collect session logs from email replies
  • Look for patterns: where did people spend the most time? Where did the codes confuse them? Did prompts get copied?
  • For revisits: attendees keep their unlock state on their own devices — they can come back and review anything they unlocked
  • For the next cohort: change at least the access code (and ideally the section codes too) so old codes don't keep working
  • Refine the module based on what you saw, then re-record the audio for your own reference
