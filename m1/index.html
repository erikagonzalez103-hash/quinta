Claude for Beginners

A grounded, peaceful introduction to building with Claude — for women who have been impressed by what's possible and are ready to try it themselves. No prior coding required.

01 ~ 5 minutes

Welcome — Why Claude?

Before we open anything, a quick word on why we're using Claude specifically and not one of the other AI tools you've heard of. And a quick check that you're set up.

Before We Begin · Tap each as you confirm
  • I have a Claude account If not — open a new tab, go to claude.ai, click Sign up. Free is enough for everything we'll do today.
  • I'm logged in to claude.ai You should see your name or initial in the top corner.
  • I'm using a modern browser Chrome, Safari, Firefox, or Edge are all fine. Internet Explorer will not work.
  • I have a separate tab or window open with claude.ai So you can switch between this module and Claude as we go.

First time? Or have you already poked around?

If this is your first time — you are exactly where you should be. We start from zero. Don't worry about anything you might have heard about coding, prompts, or AI in general. Today we just look, click, and ask.

If you've already used Claude — wonderful. You'll know some of what comes next. Stay anyway: we'll cover the home screen properly, what the modes are actually for, and where your work lives — which is the part most people get hazy on.

Why Claude (and not the others)

The phrase "learning to code" used to mean memorizing syntax, debugging errors, and reading thick textbooks. That world still exists for professional engineers. But there's a newer world — one where you describe what you want in plain English and a thoughtful collaborator builds it with you. That collaborator is Claude.

You don't need to learn to code in the traditional sense. You need to learn to direct. To know what's possible. To ask for the right thing. To recognize when something is good. That's what this module is for.

How Claude compares

Claude

  • Strongest at coding among major AI tools, by most measures
  • Live preview of what it builds, right inside the chat (Artifacts)
  • Excellent at long, careful conversations and revisions
  • Less likely to flatter you; more likely to push back

Other tools (ChatGPT, Gemini, etc.)

  • Strong general assistants — fine for many tasks
  • Good at search, image generation, and casual writing
  • Less specialized in code; previews exist but feel less polished
  • Different personalities, different defaults
You are not learning a programming language today. You are learning a working relationship with a very capable collaborator.
02 ~ 5 minutes

How Claude Works — A Short Mental Model

This section is locked

Your facilitator will share the code when we arrive here.

You don't need to understand the math. You do need to understand the shape of what's happening.

Claude is a language model — a system trained on enormous amounts of text and code from books, articles, documentation, and conversation. From all of that, it learned patterns: how sentences work, how arguments flow, how a working webpage is put together, how a thoughtful person responds to a hard question.

When you write to Claude, it considers what you've said and produces what would most usefully come next. It is not searching a database for an answer. It is generating one, fresh, in response to you.

  • Each conversation has a memory window Within one chat, Claude remembers what you've said. Open a new chat and the slate is mostly clean — though the new memory feature can carry highlights forward across chats.
  • Claude doesn't browse unless asked By default, Claude isn't on the internet. It works from what it learned during training. You can give it tools — like web search — when you need current information.
  • It can be wrong, especially about specifics Numbers, names, dates, recent events — verify these. Claude is most reliable when it's reasoning, writing, or building, less reliable when it's recalling specific facts without a source.
  • It improves with context The more clearly you describe what you want, what you don't want, who it's for, and what good looks like, the better the result.
03 ~ 3 minutes · reference

A Two-Computer Cheat Sheet

This section is locked

Your facilitator will share the code when we arrive here.

If you've used one kind of computer for years and the other one feels foreign — this is your reference. Come back to it any time.

Most of what we'll do today is the same on both: click here, type that. But shortcuts and a handful of small actions differ between Mac and PC. The single biggest thing to remember:

On a PC, the magic key is Ctrl. On a Mac, the magic key is Cmd — the one with the ⌘ symbol. For most shortcuts, that swap is the only thing that changes.

About screenshots — they are your secret weapon

One of the most useful things you can do when something looks wrong, confusing, or beautiful in your build: take a screenshot and paste it into your chat with Claude. Claude can see images and respond to them. "Why does this look like that?" "Can you make the spacing match this?" — paste the picture, ask the question.

On a Mac: press ⌘ + Shift + 4, then drag a box around what you want to capture. It saves to your desktop and copies to your clipboard automatically.

On a PC: press Win + Shift + S, then drag a box around what you want. It copies to your clipboard.

Then paste it into Claude with the paste shortcut from the table above.

A Note on the Mac Trackpad

If you've never used a Mac trackpad before, it's gestural. Tap with two fingers to right-click. Swipe with two fingers to scroll up or down. Pinch with two fingers to zoom a webpage in or out. Three-finger swipe moves between desktops. If something feels stuck, the answer is usually a two-finger gesture.

The Home Screen

Open claude.ai in a browser. We'll walk through what each part does together.

The Sidebar (left side)

  • New Chat The fresh start button. Use it when you're switching topics — keeping unrelated work in separate chats keeps each one focused.
  • Recent Chats Your conversation history. You can search across them. If you can't remember which chat you were having a thought in, search by a keyword.
  • Projects A folder for related chats — like a binder. You can give a Project shared instructions and shared documents, so Claude knows the context every time you start a new chat inside it. Use Projects when you have ongoing work: a business, a book, a course.
  • Settings Where memory, styles, and connectors live. Worth a quick visit later — not today.

Inside a Chat

  • Model selector (top of chat) Claude comes in different sizes. Opus is the most capable — slower, more thoughtful. Sonnet is the balanced default. Haiku is the fastest. For most work, the default is fine. For hard creative or strategic problems, choose Opus.
  • Tools menu Toggle web search, file creation, code execution, and more. Tools change what Claude can do in a given chat. We'll come back to these.
  • Style selector You can give Claude a custom voice — formal, casual, in your own style — that applies across chats. Optional, but lovely once tuned.
  • The Artifact panel (right side) When Claude builds something — a webpage, a document, a chart — it opens here, live, side-by-side with your chat. This is the magic.
Try It Now

Open claude.ai. Click around. Hover over each icon. Click into Settings just to peek. Don't worry about breaking anything — you cannot. Spend two minutes just exploring.

05 ~ 10 minutes

Coding Capabilities — and Where to Use Them

This section is locked

Your facilitator will share the code when we arrive here.

There is more than one way to use Claude. Knowing which version to use, when, will save you hours.

The Web Interface (claude.ai)

This is what you've already been looking at. It's a chat window with the ability to build things. For 90% of what you'll do — prototypes, simple websites, documents, learning, brainstorming — this is exactly the right tool.

Claude Code (the terminal version)

Claude Code is a more powerful version of Claude that runs on your computer in a tool called the terminal. Instead of building one file at a time in a chat window, it can read, edit, and create many files in a real project, run code, and act more like a teammate sitting next to you. It's what professional developers use for serious work.

You probably don't need this yet. Mention it exists. Come back to it once you've built three or four things in the chat interface and start to feel limited.

What people mean by "modes"

There isn't one master mode switch. Instead, Claude has a few different ways of working you can turn on:

Default Chat
Just talking with Claude — asking questions, building things, getting help. The starting point.
Research
For deeper questions that need the web. Claude searches multiple sources, reads them, and produces a longer, more thorough answer with citations. Use this for "What's the current state of [topic]?" or "Compare these five options for me."
Imagine
An exploratory mode where Claude can generate interfaces and tools on the fly — useful when you're not sure what you want and you'd like to see options.
Cowork (early access)
A desktop app where Claude can help with files on your computer directly — moving things, organizing, automating small tasks. Aimed at non-developers.
Tools (toggleable per chat)
Web search, file creation, image search, and more. Turn these on when you need them; leave them off when you don't.
Rule of Thumb

Start in the regular chat. Add tools as you need them. Move to Claude Code only once you have a real, multi-file project that's outgrown a single conversation.

06 ~ 10 minutes

Where Your Build Lives

This section is locked

Your facilitator will share the code when we arrive here.

When Claude builds you a webpage, where is it? This is the question that confuses everyone at first. Let's settle it.

There are three different places your work can live, and each one is for a different stage of its life.

1. A Claude Artifact

When Claude builds something for you in a chat, it lives inside that chat, in the Artifact panel. You can refine it, share a preview link with someone, and come back to it later. Think of an Artifact as a sketch on beautiful paper — real, viewable, but tied to the studio it was made in.

Use Artifacts for: prototyping, quick tools, sharing previews with collaborators, anything you don't need to be permanent or public.

2. GitHub

GitHub is a free home for code on the internet. Owned by Microsoft. Used by everyone from solo builders to giant companies. You upload your files there, and they're saved, version-tracked, and shareable.

GitHub also has a free service called GitHub Pages that takes your files and turns them into a real, public website at an address like yourname.github.io/your-project. This is how many simple websites you've seen are hosted.

Use GitHub when: you want your build to live on the public internet, you want to keep a history of changes, you want a real URL you can share.

3. Cloudflare

Cloudflare is a bigger, more powerful hosting and security company. It does many things, but two are most relevant to you:

  • Cloudflare Pages Like GitHub Pages, but faster and with more options. Hosts your website.
  • Cloudflare Workers Tiny pieces of code that run in the cloud, on demand. Useful when your website needs to do something more than just sit there — like fetch data from somewhere else, or process a form.

The mental model

Claude Artifact

The sketch. Viewable, refinable, shareable as a preview. Lives inside Claude.

GitHub Pages

The free public address. Your build, on the open internet, with a URL.

Cloudflare

The professional address. Faster, more capable, better for things that need to do work in the background.

Custom Domain (yourname.com)

A name you buy, then point at any of the above. Sits on top.

You don't need to choose today. Build first. Move it onto GitHub or Cloudflare only when you have something worth sharing.

Hands-On — A Personal Landing Page

Now we build something real. A simple page that introduces you. We'll do it together, one prompt at a time.

  1. Open a fresh chat Go to claude.ai. Click New Chat in the sidebar. We want a clean slate.
  2. Send your opening prompt Use the prompt below as your starting point. Replace the bracketed parts with your own details. Tap the Copy prompt button, switch to your Claude tab, and paste it in — ⌘ + V on a Mac, Ctrl + V on a PC. Then press Enter to send.
  3. Watch the Artifact appear Claude will build your page in the panel on the right. Read it. Click around. Notice what it chose for you.
  4. Refine in plain English Now ask for changes. "Make the colors warmer." "Add a section about my work." "Try a different font." Each request gets a new version. If something doesn't look right, take a screenshot (⌘ + Shift + 4 on Mac, Win + Shift + S on PC) and paste it into the chat with your question.
  5. Stop when it feels yours That's it. You've built a webpage. It's saved in the chat — you can come back to it any time.
I'd like to build a simple, beautiful personal landing page for myself. Here's about me: two or three sentences about who you are, what you do, what you care about. The feeling I want is warm and welcoming / quietly confident / playful and curious — pick one. Please build it as a single HTML artifact with elegant typography and a thoughtful color palette. Include a short introduction, a section for what I do, and a way to reach me.

If you have time — refinement prompts to try

Change the color palette to soft blues and creams. Make the headings feel more elegant.
Add a section called "Currently" with three things I'm working on or thinking about right now. Style it differently from the rest of the page.
Make the page feel less like a resume and more like an invitation. I want someone landing here to feel something.
Notice

You didn't write a single line of code. You described what you wanted in your own words and watched it become real. This is the skill. Everything else is a refinement of it.

08 ~ 5 minutes

Beyond the Basics — When You'll Need More

This section is locked

Your facilitator will share the code when we arrive here.

You can go a long way with Claude alone. Here's a glimpse of what you reach for when your idea grows up.

A landing page is content that just sits there. It looks beautiful, says what you want, and never changes. Most of what you'll build at first is exactly this — and that's wonderful.

But sometimes your idea needs to remember things. A list of clients. A login for users. Data that someone enters that needs to come back tomorrow. The moment your build needs memory across visits, you've crossed into a new territory. Welcome.

Tools that give your build memory

Supabase
A free, friendly database in a box. The most common choice for builders like you. It handles user accounts, stores your data, and gives Claude clear instructions to work with. You'll hear this name a lot. Free for small projects.
Airtable
A spreadsheet that thinks like a database. The easiest path if you already love spreadsheets. Less powerful than Supabase, but more familiar.
Firebase
Google's version of Supabase. Powerful, slightly more complex. Both are valid; people pick based on taste.
Stripe
For accepting payments. The standard. Plug it in when you're ready to charge for something.
Resend / SendGrid
For sending emails from your app. When a form on your site needs to email you, these are how.

How to know when you need them

  • You don't, until you do If your build is a beautiful page, a portfolio, a simple tool — stay in the chat with Claude. Don't pre-emptively reach for complexity.
  • The signal is "save this" The first time you find yourself wishing your build could remember a list, a login, or a record across visits — that's the moment. Ask Claude. It will walk you through it.
  • Claude knows these tools well You don't need to learn Supabase before you use it. You learn it through Claude, the same way you've been learning everything else.

A closing word

You came in thinking you needed to learn to code. You're leaving with something more useful: a clear sense of what's possible, a feel for how to ask, and a single page you built yourself.

The first three things you build will feel slow and uncertain. The next three will feel quicker. By the tenth, you'll be surprised at what you reach for naturally. Keep building. Keep asking. The skill is the asking.

You are not here to replace developers. You are here to stop being the one who has to wait for them.
